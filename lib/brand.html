Brand Positioning

For consumers who want a smarter, faster, easier way to shop for energy, Choose Energy is a marketplace where customers can compare rates, terms and energy resources and complete the switch in minutes.

Unlike competitors, we put the customer first by offering an unbiased and straightforward selection of energy services from trusted partners.

Color Usage

Typically, we will be focusing on a lighter, monochrome interface with colors providing highlights for interaction points through our primary brand colors. Our primary brand colors are Shamrock Green, Curious Blue, and Sun Orange.

Shamrock Green

Shamrock Green is our primary interaction color. Green is typically a color associated with positive interactions and growth. It should be the most commonly used color throughout our site, as it will be the default color for call-to-action buttons and default text link color.

Example usages include the following:

  • default CTA button color
  • default link color

Curious Blue

Curious Blue is our primary informational color. Blue is typically a color associated with information and calm. Generally, this color should designate informational content. In terms of frequency of use, this should be the least used of our three brand colors.

Example usages include the following:

  • non-CTA button color
  • non-CTA icon color
  • informational label color
  • informational tooltips

Sun Orange

Sun Orange is our secondary interaction color. Orange is typically a color associated with action and excitement. When using our orange palette, be careful not to overuse it in any given interface view, as its saturation and strength as a call-to-action may become distracting.

Example usages include the following:

  • CTA icon color
  • active link color
  • alert or CTA label color

Typography

Headlines for Choose Energy. Strong. Emphasis.

We use Franklin Gothic URW Demi for our headlines. The grotesque flourishes on Franklin Gothic like in the "g" and the cut of various descenders provides a distinct feel.

@include typeface(headline) or $font-family-headline + $font-weight-headline
Subheaders for Choose Energy. Strong. Emphasis.

Like our primary headlines, we use Franklin Gothic URW for our subheads; however, instead of the Demi weight, we use the Book weight for our subheads in order to provide appropriate contrast between our headlines.

@include typeface(subhead) or $font-family-subhead + $font-weight-subhead
Accent text for Choose Energy. Strong. Emphasis.

For accent copy like pull quotes and blockquotes, we use Merriweather Regular. It provides a nice contemporary serif that works well with the more modern feel of the body copy.

@include typeface(accent) or $font-family-accent + $font-weight-accent

For body copy and user interface text, we use Proxima Nova Light, a modern geometric typeface with limited flourishes to contrast with the headlines.

Voice and Tone

"If it sounds like writing, I rewrite it." — Elmore Leonard

Choose Energy's voice is human and familiar, and our tone is conversational. Think of us as the well-informed friend or neighbor you always turn to for sound advice.

We are:

  • Helpful but not salesy
  • Friendly but not intrusive
  • Casual but not lazy
  • Authoritative but not pushy
  • Confident but not cocky
  • Smart but not stodgy
  • Knowledgable but not jargony

Our unofficial tagline is "empowering choice," and that spirit should come through in every piece of content we create.

Writing Style

To borrow from the late great George Orwell:

  • Never use a long word where a short one will do.
  • If it is possible to cut a word out, always cut it out.
  • Never use the passive where you can use the active.
  • Never use a foreign phrase, a scientific word, or a jargon word if you can think of an everyday English equivalent.
  • Break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous.

Grammar and More

  • We heart the Oxford comma. Please use a comma after the penultimate item in a list of three or more items, before 'and' or 'or.'
  • Please use bulleted or numbered lists whenever possible.
  • Feel free to use contractions
  • "To be" or not to be... please avoid "to be" verbs whenever possible, they can almost always be replaced by a more precise and active verb.
    • We would not say: Bill is going to start bringing all of his books to the group session for studying.
    • We would say: Bill decided to bring all of his books to the group study session.
  • Sometimes a prepositional phrase is necessary. If you can find a way to cut out a prepositional phrase (to, of, from, about, in, on, etc.) do so.
  • Avoid suspended sentences. Avoid subordinate clauses that break up the flow of the sentence. Those clauses should be placed at the beginning of the sentence if necessary.

Word and Phrase Bank

  • Write out "carbon dioxide" instead of using CO2

Photography Guide

  • Through our photography, we want to provide a human face and emphasize the human side of energy use to allow people to feel better connected to the unfamiliar experience of switching energy providers.
  • Personalize the user experience as much as possible by using location-specific imagery
  • Use modern, relatable imagery

Art Direction

  • Photography should all be Landscape orientation, i.e. no Portrait or Square.
  • Avoid use of fire of any type that is not natural gas, i.e. no logs, no fireplaces, no bonfires, fireworks, etc.
  • Ensure every person in a photograph exudes positivity, i.e. smiling or laughing people.
  • Unless the photos provide major location context, avoid people who are participating in activities unrelated to using some form of energy, i.e. people laying in the grass, sitting on a dock, or similar. Instead, focus on people who are participating in activities related to energy use, i.e. people cooking on a gas stove (ensure the burners are lit), people watching TV/movies, people using computers/technology, people spending time together indoors.
  • Avoid grayscale photography.
  • Use high-contrast, high dynamic range photography that will look good with white text sitting over it as often as possible, i.e. don’t use a photo of a washed out pale yellow field or light blue sky with lots of white clouds.
